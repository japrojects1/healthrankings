Overview

What is Hypertension?

Hypertension, commonly known as high blood pressure, is a chronic condition in which the force of blood against artery walls is consistently too high. It is measured in millimeters of mercury (mmHg) and recorded as two numbers — systolic (pressure when the heart beats) over diastolic (pressure between beats).

Often called the "silent killer," hypertension typically has no symptoms until serious damage has already occurred. Left uncontrolled, it dramatically increases the risk of heart attack, stroke, kidney failure, and vision loss.

Important: A single high reading does not mean you have hypertension. Blood pressure naturally fluctuates throughout the day. A diagnosis requires consistently elevated readings taken on multiple occasions.

Blood Pressure Classification — American Heart Association

Normal Less than 120/80 No action needed. Maintain healthy habits.
Elevated 120–129 / less than 80 Lifestyle changes recommended.
Stage 1 130–139 / 80–89 Lifestyle changes + possible medication.
Stage 2 140+ / 90+ Medication typically required.
Crisis 180+ / 120+ Seek emergency care immediately.
Symptoms

Signs & Symptoms of High Blood Pressure

Most people with hypertension have no symptoms — which is why regular monitoring is critical. When symptoms do occur, it is often at very high levels or after years of uncontrolled pressure.

🤕

Headaches

Typically at the back of the head, especially in the morning

😵

Dizziness

Lightheadedness or feeling off-balance

👁️

Vision Changes

Blurred or double vision, seeing spots

💓

Heart Palpitations

Rapid, fluttering, or pounding heartbeat

🫁

Shortness of Breath

Especially during physical activity or at rest

🩸

Nosebleeds

Frequent or unexplained nosebleeds

Causes & Risk Factors

What Causes High Blood Pressure?

Primary hypertension (essential hypertension) has no single identifiable cause and develops gradually over years. Secondary hypertension is caused by an underlying condition and can appear suddenly with higher readings.

Age

Risk increases significantly after age 55 for men, 65 for women

Family History

Strong genetic component — first-degree relatives double your risk

Obesity

Extra weight forces the heart to work harder to circulate blood

High Sodium Diet

Excess salt causes the body to retain fluid, raising pressure

Physical Inactivity

Sedentary lifestyle increases resting heart rate and BP

Tobacco & Alcohol

Both raise BP immediately and cause long-term artery damage

Chronic Stress

Repeated stress responses keep BP elevated over time

Kidney Disease

Kidneys regulate fluid and salt balance — disease disrupts this

Lifestyle

Lifestyle Recommendations

Lifestyle changes can lower systolic blood pressure by 5–20 mmHg and may reduce or eliminate the need for medication in Stage 1 cases. These are first-line interventions recommended by all major cardiology organizations.

🥗

DASH Diet

Rich in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, and low-fat dairy. Reduces BP by 8–14 mmHg. Limit sodium to under 2,300mg/day (ideally 1,500mg).

🏃

Regular Exercise

150 minutes of moderate aerobic activity per week (brisk walking, cycling, swimming) can lower BP by 5–8 mmHg.

⚖️

Weight Management

Losing just 5–10 lbs can measurably lower BP. Every 2 lbs lost can reduce systolic pressure by about 1 mmHg.

🚭

Quit Smoking

Smoking raises BP immediately and permanently damages artery walls. Quitting is one of the highest-impact changes you can make.

🍷

Limit Alcohol

No more than 1 drink/day for women, 2 for men. Excess alcohol is directly linked to elevated BP and medication resistance.

🧘

Stress Management

Breathing exercises, meditation, and yoga reduce cortisol and sympathetic nervous system activity, lowering baseline BP.

Medications

Common Blood Pressure Medications

Most people with Stage 2 hypertension or Stage 1 with high cardiovascular risk require medication. Your doctor will select the right drug or combination based on your specific profile, other conditions, and tolerability.

Drug Class Examples How It Works Common Side Effects
ACE Inhibitors Lisinopril, Enalapril Block enzyme that narrows blood vessels Dry cough, elevated potassium
ARBs Losartan, Valsartan Block angiotensin receptors to relax vessels Dizziness, elevated potassium
Calcium Channel Blockers Amlodipine, Diltiazem Relax and widen arteries by blocking calcium Swelling in legs, flushing
Diuretics (Thiazide) Hydrochlorothiazide, Chlorthalidone Help kidneys remove excess sodium and water Frequent urination, low potassium
Beta-Blockers Metoprolol, Atenolol Slow heart rate and reduce cardiac output Fatigue, cold extremities

Never stop medication without talking to your doctor. Abruptly stopping some blood pressure drugs can cause rebound hypertension — a sudden, dangerous spike in blood pressure.

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

How often should I check my blood pressure at home?
The American Heart Association recommends checking twice daily — once in the morning before taking medication and before eating or drinking, and once in the evening. Take two to three readings one minute apart each session and record the average. Do this for a week before any doctor's appointment.
Are home blood pressure monitors accurate?
Clinically validated home monitors are as accurate as those used in a doctor's office. The key is proper technique — sit quietly for 5 minutes before measuring, keep your arm at heart level, use the correct cuff size, and avoid caffeine or exercise within 30 minutes. All five monitors on our list have passed independent clinical validation protocols.
What cuff size do I need?
Cuff size is critical for accuracy. Measure the circumference of your upper arm at its midpoint. Small/child: under 9 inches. Standard adult: 9–13 inches. Large adult: 13–17 inches. Extra-large: 17–21 inches. Using the wrong size — typically too small — is the most common cause of inaccurate home readings, usually resulting in falsely elevated numbers.
My reading at home is different from my doctor's office. Which is right?
Both can be right — they are often measuring different things. White coat hypertension (anxiety-elevated readings in a clinical setting) is real and affects up to 20% of patients. Home readings are often considered more representative of true baseline BP. Conversely, masked hypertension is when home readings are higher than clinical ones. Bring your home monitor to your next appointment and compare readings side by side with your doctor.
Can a blood pressure monitor detect atrial fibrillation (AFib)?
Some advanced monitors, including the Omron Platinum on our list, include an irregular heartbeat detection feature that can flag possible AFib. However, these devices are not diagnostic tools — they should be considered early warning indicators. If your monitor flags an irregular heartbeat, contact your doctor for a confirmatory ECG before drawing any conclusions.
Do I still need to see a doctor if I monitor at home?
Absolutely. Home monitoring is a complement to — not a replacement for — professional medical care. It helps you and your doctor make better-informed decisions about your treatment, track how well your medication is working, and catch dangerous spikes early. Share your home logs at every appointment.
